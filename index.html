Why The Popular Alternatives Fail

Ranked Choice Voting has three fatal flaws:

1. Hidden Support: RCV has the "later-no-harm" flaw, meaning eliminated candidates' second choices are never counted or revealed. A Green candidate's elimination hides that they were the second choice of 40% of voters, making third parties look weaker than they are.

2. Center Squeeze: Broadly appealing candidates get eliminated unfairly. A centrist who could beat anyone head-to-head gets squeezed out by more polarized candidates with stronger bases.

3. Strategic Voting Returns: Voters start gaming the system again, voting for "safer" candidates instead of their true preferences.

Australia proves this: Australia's House of Representatives uses single-winner RCV (called "preferential voting") and remains essentially two-party dominated after 150+ years. The Liberal/National Coalition and Labor Party have controlled 95%+ of House seats since World War II. Meanwhile, Australia's Senate uses proportional representation and successfully elects multiple parties‚Äîbut proportional representation is federally banned for US Congress and faces massive political obstacles. Single-winner RCV, which could theoretically be adopted in the US, demonstrably fails to break two-party dominance even after more than a century of use.
